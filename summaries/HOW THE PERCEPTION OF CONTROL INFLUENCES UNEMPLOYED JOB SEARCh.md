## Research Question
*What problem or question does this paper address?*

How does locus of control—the degree to which one believes one's actions influence outcomes—affect unemployed job seekers' search behavior, specifically their reservation wages and search intensity? The paper addresses whether "internal" job seekers (who believe their actions determine outcomes) behave differently than "external" job seekers (who believe their actions have little effect on outcomes).

## Methodology
*How did the researchers approach the problem?*

The author incorporates locus of control into a discrete-time, infinite-horizon model of unemployed job search and tests predictions using data from the National Longitudinal Survey of Youth (NLSY79) from 1979-1986. The study analyzes the relationship between locus of control measures and job search outcomes including reservation wages, search intensity, and unemployment duration.

## Key Findings
*What are the main results or conclusions?*

- Internal job seekers set reservation wages that are 1.1% higher per standard deviation increase in internality compared to external job seekers
- Internal job seekers search more intensively (an estimated 0.42-hour increase in weekly search hours), though this finding is statistically imprecise
- Internal job seekers are no better at converting search effort into job offers and earn no more than external job seekers upon finding employment

## Core Argument
*What is the central thesis or contribution?*

Locus of control influences job search behavior through its effect on beliefs about the return to search effort—internal job seekers believe their efforts are more likely to generate job offers, leading them to search more intensively and set higher reservation wages. This psychological factor has effects on search behavior comparable to conventional human capital measures, suggesting that understanding personality traits could inform more targeted employment assistance programs.

## Evidence & Support
*What data or reasoning supports the claims?*

The analysis uses NLSY79 data with measures of locus of control, reservation wages, and search experiences of unemployed respondents, including detailed work-history information. The findings are supported by the fact that internal workers don't actually perform better in job search outcomes, confirming that the effect operates through beliefs rather than actual ability differences.

## Critical Notes

### Strengths

- Provides both theoretical model and empirical evidence linking psychological traits to concrete economic behaviors
- Uses comprehensive longitudinal data allowing examination of unemployment durations and re-employment wages beyond just search behavior

### Limitations

- Some key findings (search intensity effects) are statistically imprecise
- Sample may have selection issues that related work on newly unemployed German workers was able to avoid

### Open Questions
*What remains unresolved or needs further investigation?*

- Whether locus of control beliefs can be effectively modified through interventions or if policy should focus on adapting assistance to existing traits
- How to optimally design targeted employment programs based on personality characteristics like locus of control

### Related Papers
*Key works cited in the introduction that this paper builds upon:*

- **Coleman and Deliere (2003)** — Established that locus of control influences educational attainment through effects on beliefs, providing precedent for the mechanism proposed in this study
- **Heckman, Stixrud, and Urzua (2006)** — Demonstrated that noncognitive characteristics including personality traits are related to job search decisions, motivating the focus on psychological factors
- **Caliendo, Cobb-Clark, and Uhlendorff (forthcoming)** — Independent complementary work finding similar relationships between locus of control and reservation wages/search behavior among German job seekers
- **McGee and McGee (2011)** — Laboratory experiment showing locus of control relates to search behavior only under uncertainty about returns to search effort, supporting the theoretical mechanism
- **Spinnewijn (2009)** — Found that job searchers overestimate arrival rates of job offers independent of search effort, establishing that beliefs about search processes affect behavior

## Summary
*2-3 sentence overview in your own words*

This study shows that unemployed job seekers' beliefs about whether their actions influence outcomes significantly affect their job search behavior. Internal job seekers who believe their efforts matter set higher wage expectations and search more intensively, though they don't actually find jobs faster or earn more, suggesting the effect operates through beliefs rather than actual differences in search ability.
