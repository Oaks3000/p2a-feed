# A Garbage Can Model of Organizational Choice

## Research Question
*What problem or question does this paper address?*

How do organizations make decisions when they lack clear goals, well-defined processes, and stable participation? The paper specifically examines decision-making in "organized anarchies" - organizations characterized by problematic preferences, unclear technology, and fluid participation.

## Methodology
*How did the researchers approach the problem?*

The researchers developed a computer simulation model called the "garbage can model" that treats organizational decision-making as the intersection of four independent streams: problems, solutions, participants, and choice opportunities. They used this model to examine decision processes in organizations like universities.

## Key Findings
*What are the main results or conclusions?*

- Organizations often function as "collections of choices looking for problems, issues and feelings looking for decision situations, solutions looking for issues to which they might be an answer, and decision makers looking for work"
- Decision-making in organized anarchies involves the relatively independent flow of four streams (problems, solutions, participants, choice opportunities) that intersect in unpredictable ways
- The timing of when problems, solutions, and participants converge around choice opportunities significantly affects organizational outcomes, rather than following rational sequential decision-making processes

## Core Argument
*What is the central thesis or contribution?*

Traditional models of rational organizational decision-making inadequately describe how many organizations actually operate, particularly those with ambiguous goals and unclear processes. The garbage can model better captures the chaotic, temporal nature of organizational choice where solutions often precede problems and decision outcomes depend more on timing and coincidence than systematic analysis.

## Evidence & Support
*What data or reasoning supports the claims?*

The authors base their model on seven recent studies of universities and draw on behavioral theories of organizations. They support their arguments through computer simulation results and empirical observations of how universities and similar organizations actually make decisions.

## Critical Notes

### Strengths

- Provides a novel and realistic alternative to rational choice models of organizational decision-making
- Offers insights into how organizations function despite apparent chaos and ambiguity

### Limitations

- The model is primarily descriptive and offers limited prescriptive guidance for managers
- Focuses heavily on universities and similar organizations, potentially limiting generalizability to other organizational types

### Open Questions
*What remains unresolved or needs further investigation?*

- How can managers effectively lead and coordinate in organized anarchies if traditional management theories assume clear goals and stable participation?
- What constitutes "intelligent" decision-making when organizational goals are unclear or unknown?
