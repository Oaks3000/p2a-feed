# Locus Of Control And Beliefs About Superstition And Luck In Adolescents: What's Their Relationship?

## Citation

> **Authors:** Elisabetta Sagone, Maria Elvira De Caroli

## Research Question
*What problem or question does this paper address?*

What is the relationship between locus of control and personal beliefs about superstition and luck in Sicilian middle and late adolescents? The study also examines age and sex differences in these psychological constructs.

## Methodology
*How did the researchers approach the problem?*

The researchers used a correlational design with 118 Sicilian adolescents (ages 14-19) from two public high schools, administering self-report questionnaires including locus of control scales, superstition and luck belief scales, and semantic differentials.

## Key Findings
*What are the main results or conclusions?*

- Late adolescents showed more internal locus of control while middle adolescents were more externally oriented and more superstitious
- Internal locus of control was negatively associated with superstitious beliefs, while external locus of control was positively associated with beliefs in both superstition and good luck
- Middle adolescents believed more strongly in both superstition and good luck compared to late adolescents

## Core Argument
*What is the central thesis or contribution?*

Adolescents' sense of personal control over events is systematically related to their supernatural beliefs, with those who feel less in control of their lives being more likely to rely on superstition and luck. This relationship changes with developmental maturation from middle to late adolescence.

## Evidence & Support
*What data or reasoning supports the claims?*

The findings are supported by correlational analyses showing significant relationships between locus of control orientation and supernatural beliefs across 118 participants. The study used validated psychological scales to measure these constructs systematically.

## Critical Notes

### Strengths

- Uses well-established, validated psychological instruments for measuring key constructs
- Addresses an understudied population (adolescents) in research on superstition and locus of control

### Limitations

- Relatively small sample size (118 participants) limits generalizability beyond the specific cultural context
- Cross-sectional design prevents determination of causal relationships between locus of control and supernatural beliefs

### Open Questions
*What remains unresolved or needs further investigation?*

- How do personality factors and resilience influence the relationships between control beliefs and supernatural thinking?
- What are the causal mechanisms underlying the observed correlations between locus of control and superstitious beliefs?

### Related Papers
*Key works cited in the introduction that this paper builds upon:*

- **Rotter (1966)** — Established the foundational theory of internal versus external locus of control, distinguishing between beliefs that events result from personal behavior versus chance/luck
- **Darke & Freedman (1997)** — Developed scales measuring beliefs in superstition and good luck, showing these beliefs relate to external locus of control and remain stable over time
- **Vyse (1997)** — Argued that superstitious behavior emerges from uncertainty in random or uncontrollable circumstances, providing theoretical framework for current study
- **Damisch, Stoberock, & Mussweiler (2010)** — Demonstrated experimentally that activating good-luck superstitions can improve performance through enhanced self-efficacy
- **Peterson (1978)** — Conducted early research showing individuals with external locus of control were more likely to believe in self-oriented superstitions

## Summary
*2-3 sentence overview in your own words*

This study found that adolescents with external locus of control (believing events are determined by chance rather than personal action) are more likely to believe in superstition and luck. The relationship between personal control beliefs and supernatural thinking appears to strengthen with age, as older adolescents show more internal control and less superstitious thinking.
