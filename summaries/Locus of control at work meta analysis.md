## Research Question
*What problem or question does this paper address?*

This paper addresses how locus of control (LOC) - the extent to which people believe they have control over their own fate - relates to various work outcomes. The researchers sought to provide a comprehensive quantitative summary of these relationships across a wide range of workplace criteria.

## Methodology
*How did the researchers approach the problem?*

The researchers conducted a meta-analysis to quantitatively summarize relationships between locus of control and work outcomes. They categorized outcomes according to three theoretical perspectives: LOC and well-being, LOC and motivation, and LOC and behavioral orientation.

## Key Findings
*What are the main results or conclusions?*

- Internal locus of control was positively associated with favorable work outcomes including positive task and social experiences
- Individuals with internal LOC demonstrated greater job motivation compared to those with external LOC
- Internal LOC was linked to better general well-being, job satisfaction, and job performance outcomes

## Core Argument
*What is the central thesis or contribution?*

The central thesis is that locus of control has broad and systematic effects on employees' organizational lives, with internal LOC consistently predicting more favorable outcomes. The authors argue that LOC operates through three distinct theoretical mechanisms affecting well-being, motivation, and behavioral orientation at work.

## Evidence & Support
*What data or reasoning supports the claims?*

The authors synthesized quantitative findings across multiple studies using meta-analytic techniques to control for measurement error and sampling biases. They built on previous qualitative reviews and limited quantitative analyses to provide comprehensive effect size estimates.

### Related Papers
*Key works cited in the introduction that this paper builds upon:*

- **Rotter (1966)** — Established the foundational distinction between internal and external locus of control, defining internals as those who believe they control their fate versus externals who attribute outcomes to external factors
- **Spector (1982)** — Provided the first comprehensive qualitative review demonstrating that internals have higher job motivation, performance, satisfaction, and leadership, establishing LOC's broad workplace relevance
- **Judge & Bono (2001)** — Conducted a meta-analysis showing internal LOC related to job satisfaction (r = 0.32) and job performance (r = 0.22), and proposed LOC as a component of core self-evaluation
- **Judge et al. (1998)** — Developed the core self-evaluation construct that includes LOC as one of four components along with self-esteem, self-efficacy, and emotional stability
- **Bono & Judge (2003)** — Advanced the theoretical understanding of LOC within the core self-evaluation framework and its relationship to Big Five personality traits
