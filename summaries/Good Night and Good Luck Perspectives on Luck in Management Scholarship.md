# Good Night, and Good Luck: Perspectives on Luck in Management Scholarship

## Research Question
*What problem or question does this paper address?*

How is the concept of luck understood and applied in management scholarship, and what are the different perspectives through which researchers conceptualize luck's role in explaining organizational and individual performance differences?

## Methodology
*How did the researchers approach the problem?*

The researchers conducted a systematic literature review of luck in management scholarship and related disciplines (economics, sociology, psychology). They analyzed the use of "luck" in six leading management journals and categorized different conceptual approaches to understanding luck.

## Key Findings
*What are the main results or conclusions?*

- Only 2% of articles in leading management journals included the word "luck" in their title, abstract, or main text, despite its theoretical importance
- Five distinct perspectives on luck emerge from the literature: Attribution, Randomness, Counterfactual, Undeserved, and Serendipity
- People systematically underestimate good luck in successes while recognizing bad luck in failures, creating an asymmetry in performance evaluations

## Core Argument
*What is the central thesis or contribution?*

While luck has been acknowledged as an important factor in explaining performance differences by seminal management scholars, it remains underexplored and inconsistently conceptualized. The authors propose that understanding different perspectives on luck can advance management theory and address practical issues like executive compensation and social inequality through a new "luck as Leveler" perspective.

## Evidence & Support
*What data or reasoning supports the claims?*

The analysis is supported by a quantitative review of luck usage across six major management journals and a comprehensive examination of how luck is conceptualized across management and foundational disciplines. The authors draw on examples like Honda's Supercub success and various theoretical contributions that explicitly reference luck.

## Critical Notes

### Strengths

- Provides the first systematic review of luck concepts across management literature and related disciplines
- Offers a clear typology of different luck perspectives that can guide future research

### Limitations

- Limited to published academic literature, potentially missing practical applications of luck concepts
- The review may not capture all indirect references to luck-related concepts that use alternative terminology

### Open Questions
*What remains unresolved or needs further investigation?*

- How can the asymmetry between luck attribution in successes versus failures be better explained and addressed in organizational contexts?
- What are the practical implications of implementing "luck as Leveler" perspectives in addressing executive compensation and social inequality?

### Related Papers
*Key works cited in the introduction that this paper builds upon:*

- **Mintzberg, Pascale, Rumelt, & Goold (1996)** — Debated Honda's Supercub motorcycle success, with Mintzberg arguing that luck was an important explanatory factor at the micro level
- **Rumelt (1991)** — Highlighted the predominance of random variations at the macro level in explaining firm performance differences
- **Barney (1986)** — One of several seminal management contributions that explicitly referenced luck as an explanation for performance differences
- **Denrell (2004)** — Argued that randomness in structured environments can produce systematic patterns, making luck essential for explaining performance differences
- **Collins & Hansen (2011)** — Proposed that while good and bad luck can happen to anyone, some organizations are better prepared to secure higher "return on luck"
