## Research Question
*What problem or question does this paper address?*

How does locus of control operate through people's preferences and beliefs to influence their decision-making? Specifically, the paper examines whether locus of control affects decisions through preference for agency, optimism and confidence about returns to effort, or illusion of control.

## Methodology
*How did the researchers approach the problem?*

The researchers conducted a laboratory experiment using the delegation game framework, where principals decide whether to retain or delegate investment decisions to agents. They used a stated-effort design to eliminate confounding factors related to individual differences in effort costs and productivity.

## Key Findings
*What are the main results or conclusions?*

- Internal locus of control is linked to a stronger preference for agency, with this effect being driven primarily by women
- No evidence was found that locus of control influences optimism or confidence about returns to stated effort
- No evidence was found that locus of control operates through an illusion of control mechanism

## Core Argument
*What is the central thesis or contribution?*

Locus of control operates as a preference parameter, specifically through people's intrinsic preference for maintaining agency over their decisions. This represents a new behavioral channel distinct from the previously emphasized role of locus of control in shaping expectations about returns to effort.

## Evidence & Support
*What data or reasoning supports the claims?*

The findings are supported by experimental data from delegation games where participants' decisions to retain versus delegate control were measured alongside their locus of control scores. The stated-effort design allowed researchers to isolate preference-based mechanisms from productivity-based explanations.

## Critical Notes

### Strengths

- Controlled experimental design eliminates confounding factors present in observational studies
- Novel investigation of preference for agency as a mechanism linking locus of control to outcomes

### Limitations

- Stated-effort design may not fully capture real-world decision-making contexts
- Limited generalizability beyond laboratory settings where effort costs and productivity vary

### Open Questions
*What remains unresolved or needs further investigation?*

- How do these laboratory findings translate to real-world decision-making contexts with variable effort costs?
- What explains the gender differences observed in the preference for agency effect?

### Related Papers
*Key works cited in the introduction that this paper builds upon:*

- **Rotter (1954, 1966)** — Established the foundational concept of locus of control as people's beliefs about whether reinforcement is contingent on their own behavior versus external forces
- **Bartling et al. (2014)** — Introduced the delegation game framework that this study uses to measure preference for agency in controlled laboratory settings
- **Caliendo et al. (2015)** — Demonstrated how economists typically relate locus of control to decision-making through expectations about returns to effort, providing the baseline theory this study extends
- **Spector (1982)** — Established that internals not only perceive greater control but actively seek situations where control is possible, motivating the preference for agency hypothesis
- **McGee & McGee (2016)** — Provided conceptual framework for measuring how locus of control links to decision-making under uncertainty in laboratory settings

## Summary
*2-3 sentence overview in your own words*

This laboratory experiment finds that people with an internal locus of control have a stronger preference for maintaining control over their own decisions, particularly among women. However, locus of control does not appear to influence optimism, confidence, or create illusions of control as previously theorized.
