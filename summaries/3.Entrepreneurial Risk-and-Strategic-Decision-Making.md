## Research Question
*What problem or question does this paper address?*

Why do entrepreneurs consistently engage in risky business ventures despite empirical evidence showing they don't have higher risk propensity than the general population? This paper addresses the paradox between entrepreneurs' actual risky behavior and their measured risk-taking preferences.

## Methodology
*How did the researchers approach the problem?*

The researchers examined entrepreneurial risk through the lens of cognitive psychology and decision making, comparing how entrepreneurs versus managers in large organizations use biases and heuristics in their decision-making processes.

## Key Findings
*What are the main results or conclusions?*

- Entrepreneurs use representativeness heuristics more extensively in their decision making compared to managers in large organizations
- Entrepreneurs demonstrate significantly higher levels of overconfidence than managers in large organizations
- Entrepreneurs perceive less risk in business situations not because they have higher risk propensity, but because their cognitive biases lead them to underestimate actual risks

## Core Argument
*What is the central thesis or contribution?*

The central thesis is that entrepreneurial risk-taking can be explained by entrepreneurs' increased use of cognitive biases and heuristics, which leads them to perceive less risk in business opportunities rather than having an inherently higher tolerance for risk. This cognitive perspective resolves the longstanding paradox in entrepreneurship research.

## Evidence & Support
*What data or reasoning supports the claims?*

The argument is supported by empirical data showing entrepreneurs use representativeness more and exhibit greater overconfidence than organizational managers. The evidence builds on cognitive psychology research demonstrating how biases and heuristics systematically influence risk perception and decision making.

### Related Papers
*Key works cited in the introduction that this paper builds upon:*

- **Brockhaus (1980)** — Conducted the most widely cited study showing entrepreneurs' risk propensity does not differ significantly from the general population, establishing the central paradox this paper aims to resolve
- **Cooper, Dunkelberg, & Woo (1988)** — Found that entrepreneurs perceive their chances for success to be much higher than competitors in the same industries, supporting the idea that entrepreneurs have different risk perceptions
- **Palich & Bagby (1995)** — Discovered that entrepreneurs tend to cognitively categorize business situations more positively and don't view themselves as risk takers, providing early cognitive perspective evidence
- **Tversky & Kahneman (1974)** — Established foundational research on how people use biases and heuristics in decision making, departing from rational decision models
- **McClelland (1961)** — Influential early work characterizing entrepreneurs as moderate risk takers, part of the psychological tradition that failed to find higher risk propensity in entrepreneurs

## Summary
*2-3 sentence overview in your own words*

This paper resolves a key paradox in entrepreneurship research by showing that entrepreneurs don't actually have higher risk tolerance, but rather use cognitive biases that make risky ventures appear less risky to them. The study demonstrates that entrepreneurs rely more heavily on mental shortcuts and overconfidence, leading them to underestimate the actual risks in their business decisions.
